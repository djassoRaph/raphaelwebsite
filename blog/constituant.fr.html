Constituant: Building Democracy 2.0 While Admitting It's Broken

December 25, 2025 Civic Tech Democracy AI Politics France

The Honest Pitch

I'm building Constituant, a platform where French citizens can vote on real legislation from the European Parliament and National Assembly. Yes, anonymously. Yes, using AI summaries. And yes, I know exactly how broken that sounds.


But here's the thing: I'm not pretending this is perfect. I'm not claiming to have solved democracy. I'm building something that acknowledges its own limitations upfront while still trying to push the conversation forward.

For those outside of the French political system, this might seem like a radical idea. But for those who've seen how democracy has been co-opted by special interests and opaque lobbying, it's a necessary experiment.

For those outside of the IT world, what I mainly see is it seems like a real problem when it comes to security, voting influence and real data.

The Elephant in the Room

This is an experiment in direct democracy with known flaws:

  • Votes are IP-based (VPNs exist, we know)
  • Summaries are AI-generated (human moderation coming)
  • This doesn't replace real democracy (it's a thermometer for public opinion)
  • Your vote helps build the case for formal citizen assemblies

But then again, in every democracy you hear about voter fraud, and humans seem to easy to influence and corrupt. You throw them garbage on TV, and make them believe the last thing and they'll vote for the first kiddo on the block...

See? I said it first. Now we can actually talk about what this is trying to do.


The Name: Constituant

Let's define things, "constituant" means two things:

Constituant (noun): A constituent, a voter, someone who participates in democracy.
Constituant (adjective): Constitution-making, the act of creating fundamental governance.

That duality is the entire point. We start as passive voters (Phase 1), but the long-term vision is becoming active constitution-makers (Phase 3). The Critical Feedback That Changed Everything

I recently got some brutal (and correct) criticism about the project. The core issue: You're building a popularity contest, not informed democracy.

Showing people bills and letting them vote assumes citizens can make informed decisions with just an AI summary. That's false. Most legislation needs context: What problem does it solve? What are the trade-offs? What did experts say? What are the unintended consequences?

A plain language summary of a tax bill doesn't give citizens the tools to meaningfully evaluate it. You're creating the illusion of participation.

Eugh.

The Long Game: Association Loi 1901

Eventually, I want to formalize this as a French association with two tiers:

Free tier (anonymous): Vote on all bills, view results, submit corrections

Member tier (‚Ç¨12/year, identity verified): Weighted voting in association governance, propose priority bills for education content, early access to sortition experiments, create a community.

The paid tier funds content creation - articles, videos, podcasts explaining legislation. The free tier drives volume. But I won't launch the association until I have:

  • 1,000+ monthly active voters
  • 50+ bills with completed votes
  • 10+ community moderators actively contributing
  • Clear evidence people are sharing results

I'll also adjust the price depending on the price of the API calls to the usage of IA to intepret law/bills...

The association should formalize an existing community, not create one from scratch.


The Agora Vision

Here's the philosophical that came to mind while reflecting on this: now that machines can do most of the work (automation isn't slavery, it's liberation), we have more time for the agora - discussing, deliberating, engaging in actual democracy.

The AI fetches bills, summarizes them, tallies votes. Humans debate, refine, engage representatives. We're not replacing human judgment with AI - we're freeing human judgment from tedious tasks.

The Uncomfortable Truth

Someone asked me: "Is this tool to change democracy, or is it therapy for your frustration with democracy?"

Honestly? Maybe both. But at least I'm building something instead of just complaining. And I'm being honest about what it is and isn't.

I don't lack ways to express my opinions on legislation. I lack:

  • Trust in our institutions
  • I want accountability mechanisms for (corrupt?) representatives
  • Ways to deliberate with fellow citizens across differences
  • Transparency into how legislation is made and who influences it

A voting platform alone doesn't solve these. But combined with transparency, community moderation, direct representative engagement, and eventually proper sortition experiments? Maybe we can move the needle.


Why I'm Writing This

If it helps train even a small group of citizens to engage more critically with legislation and hold people accountable? That's progress.
