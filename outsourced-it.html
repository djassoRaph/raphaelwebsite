What "no IT department" actually costs you

You run a business. Something breaks. You spend an hour Googling it, another hour trying a fix that doesn't work, then call someone who charges you a callout fee to do in 20 minutes what they've done a hundred times before.

Or you don't call anyone. You work around the problem. The broken printer stays broken. The backup that "should be running" hasn't run in four months. The email that occasionally bounces gets ignored until a client mentions they never received something important.

The cost isn't the callout fee. It's the distraction, the risk, and the compounding small failures. What you actually need is someone who already knows your setup — who you can reach before the situation becomes a crisis, and who'll tell you honestly what's worth fixing and what isn't.

What outsourced IT support looks like in practice

Someone to call when things break

Email, phone, or message. You explain what's wrong in plain language. I fix it — remotely in most cases — and explain what happened.

Remote access support

With your permission, I can take control of the screen and fix the problem directly. No waiting for someone to drive to your office.

Problems caught before they happen

Expired certificates, failing backups, outdated software — I check these before they become the reason your site goes down at 9am on a Monday.

A second set of eyes on your setup

Experience means knowing what questions to ask. You don't know what you don't know — that's exactly what I bring. A broader view, a different angle.

The boring stuff, handled

Printer drivers. Software updates. Password resets. New employee setup. The things that aren't glamorous but stop your team cold when they break.

Predictable monthly cost

No surprise invoices. A fixed retainer means you know exactly what IT support costs — and you stop dreading the call you need to make.

Is this right for you?

This is for you if...

  • You have 2–40 employees and no dedicated IT person
  • Tech problems eat into your focus and your team's time
  • You've paid an emergency callout fee at least once
  • You're not sure your backups are actually working
  • You want one person who knows your setup and is reachable
  • You'd rather pay a predictable monthly fee than be surprised

This is not for you if...

  • You already have an in-house IT team — they're the right call
  • You need on-site visits more than once a month (we can discuss)
  • You want 24/7 SLA-guaranteed enterprise support with a helpdesk
  • You're looking for someone to hand everything to and never think about IT again — some involvement on your side is always needed

"Can't AI just handle this for me?"

Yes — and no. AI tools are genuinely useful, and I use them too. But AI is only as good as what you tell it. If you don't know your setup has a security gap, you won't ask about it. If you don't know what questions to ask, you'll get confident-sounding answers to the wrong questions.

Experience isn't knowing the answers. It's knowing which questions matter — and noticing the ones nobody thought to ask. That's what twenty years of fixing other people's IT problems teaches you.

Common questions

What does outsourced IT support actually mean for a small business?

It means having a senior engineer you can call or email when something breaks — without hiring a full-time employee or calling an expensive agency. You get a fixed monthly cost, remote support, and someone who already knows your setup.

My business is small. Do I really need this?

If you've ever lost hours fixing a tech problem yourself, paid a large fee for an emergency fix, or worried about what would happen if your email or website went down — then yes. The retainer costs less than one hour of emergency callout per month.

Is this the same as hiring an IT agency?

No. With an agency you pay for overhead, account managers, and junior staff who rotate. With a retainer, you work directly with one senior engineer who knows your business. No ticket queues. No escalation chains. Just a direct line.

What kind of problems can you fix remotely?

Most day-to-day IT problems: email issues, software updates, backup failures, website problems, slow systems, printer drivers, user account access, security questions, and more. Remote access tools mean I can often fix things faster than someone on-site.
