Why IT contracts became the default; and why they don't have to be

Most IT agencies require 12-month contracts. Not because that's what clients need; because that's what the agency needs to cover its overhead, predict revenue, and justify the cost of its account management team.

You sign in January when something went wrong and you were desperate. By March the problems are solved and you're paying for a service you barely use. By October you've forgotten to cancel before the auto-renewal kicks in.

The contract protects the agency, not you. A senior engineer working directly with small businesses doesn't carry that overhead. The arrangement stays in place because it's worth it every month; or it doesn't.

Why no lock-in actually works better for you

When there's no contract forcing you to stay, the relationship only continues if it's working. That keeps me focused on being actually useful; not on just meeting a minimum service level that's buried in a document you signed eighteen months ago.

Common questions

Can I get IT support without signing a long-term contract?

Yes. Plans are month-to-month; there is no minimum term and no cancellation fee. You pay for the month you use. If it works, you continue. If it doesn't, you stop. No notice period required.

Why do most IT agencies require long contracts?

Because their model depends on locking in revenue to cover overhead account managers, sales teams, junior staff. A senior engineer working directly with clients doesn't have that overhead, and doesn't need to lock you in to make the numbers work.

What happens if I need to stop the support?

You stop. There is no cancellation fee, no notice period, and no penalty. At the end of your billing month, the arrangement ends. I'll make sure you have everything you need to hand off cleanly.

Is no-contract IT support reliable?

Reliability comes from the person, not the contract. A contract doesn't make someone better at fixing your systems; experience and familiarity do. The goal is to earn your business every month by being genuinely useful; not to hold you in place with legal terms.

What does remote IT support without a contract actually include?

The same things as any good IT support: help when something breaks, routine monitoring, backup verification, security checks, software updates, new staff setup, email issues, website problems, and more. The absence of a contract doesn't mean a reduced service; it means a better relationship.
